How Restrictive Abortion Laws Create Double Burden for Women's Health

Imagine being denied both the ability to prevent an unwanted pregnancy and the option to safely terminate one if it occurs. This double jeopardy isn’t hypothetical—it’s the reality for millions of women across Sub-Saharan Africa, according to groundbreaking new research that examined reproductive health policies across 31 African countries.

The study, published in PLOS Global Public Health, analyzed data from over 453,000 women and found that those living in countries with the most restrictive abortion laws were significantly less likely to use modern contraceptives. This counterintuitive finding challenges the assumption that when abortion access is limited, contraceptive use would naturally increase to prevent unwanted pregnancies.

The Research Question That Matters

For decades, policymakers and health experts have debated a fundamental question: when countries restrict abortion access, do women compensate by using more contraceptives? The answer, as this comprehensive study reveals, is surprisingly and troublingly no.

Dr. Maxwell Tii Kumbeni and his research team at Oregon State University set out to understand this complex relationship by examining how different levels of abortion restrictions across Sub-Saharan Africa correlate with women’s contraceptive choices. What they discovered has profound implications for reproductive health policy across the developing world.

The Stark Reality of Reproductive Health in Africa

The numbers paint a sobering picture of reproductive healthcare in Sub-Saharan Africa. Only 23% of women in the study used modern contraceptives—far below the global average of 44%. Even more concerning, fewer than one in ten women used highly effective long-acting reversible contraceptives (LARC) or permanent methods like IUDs, implants, or sterilization.

Perhaps most striking: 88% of the women studied lived in countries where abortion laws were either moderately or highly restrictive, meaning abortion was only permitted in cases of rape, incest, fetal abnormalities, threats to maternal health, or to save a woman’s life.

Countries That Support Women See Better Outcomes

The research revealed a powerful finding about policy environments that support women’s reproductive autonomy. Countries with laws allowing adolescents to access contraceptives without parental or spousal consent showed dramatically higher contraceptive use rates. Women in these supportive policy environments were 55% more likely to use modern contraceptives and 130% more likely to use highly effective LARC methods compared to countries with no such protections.

This suggests that comprehensive reproductive health policies—not just individual access to services—play a crucial role in empowering women to make informed choices about their bodies and futures.

The Economics of Reproductive Health

The study also uncovered important economic factors influencing contraceptive access. Countries that invested more heavily in healthcare (measured as a percentage of gross domestic product) consistently showed higher rates of contraceptive use among women. This relationship underscores how public health investment directly translates to improved reproductive outcomes.

Countries like Namibia (50% modern contraceptive use), Lesotho (49%), and South Africa (48%) led the region in contraceptive adoption, while many nations struggled with rates below 15%.

Policy Implications: A Path Forward

The research findings point toward several evidence-based policy solutions that could dramatically improve reproductive health outcomes across Africa:

Liberalizing Abortion Laws: The data strongly suggests that restrictive abortion laws don’t reduce demand for pregnancy termination—they simply make it more dangerous while simultaneously reducing contraceptive access. Countries that liberalize abortion laws often see improved overall reproductive health outcomes.

Supporting Youth Access: Policies that eliminate barriers for young people to access contraceptives without parental or spousal consent prove highly effective at increasing overall contraceptive use. This approach recognizes that reproductive decisions are often controlled by others in young women’s lives, particularly in traditional societies.

Investing in Healthcare Infrastructure: The correlation between health expenditure and contraceptive use suggests that robust public health investment creates the foundation for improved reproductive outcomes.

Comprehensive Reproductive Health Services: Rather than viewing abortion and contraception as competing priorities, the evidence supports integrated approaches that recognize these services as complementary components of reproductive healthcare.

Who Benefits Most from Reform?

Several groups stand to gain significantly from abortion law liberalization and improved contraceptive access:

Young Women: Adolescents and young adults, who often face the greatest barriers to reproductive autonomy, would benefit from policies supporting independent access to contraceptives.

Rural Communities: The study found that rural women had lower access to modern contraceptives, suggesting targeted interventions in rural areas could have substantial impact.

Lower-Income Families: Women in the poorest wealth categories showed the lowest contraceptive use rates, indicating that affordable, accessible services could reduce health disparities.

Healthcare Systems: Reduced rates of unsafe abortion and unintended pregnancy would decrease maternal morbidity and mortality, reducing strain on already stretched healthcare resources.

The Broader Picture: Breaking the Cycle

The relationship between abortion restrictions and contraceptive access represents more than just healthcare policy—it reflects broader questions about women’s autonomy, economic opportunity, and societal development. When women can plan their families, they’re more likely to complete education, participate in the workforce, and contribute to economic growth.

Research consistently shows that unintended pregnancies contribute to psychological distress, adverse birth outcomes, and long-term socioeconomic challenges for families. By contrast, when women have access to comprehensive reproductive health services, entire communities benefit from improved health outcomes and economic stability.

Looking Ahead: The Promise of Evidence-Based Policy

This research adds to a growing body of evidence that restrictive abortion laws don’t achieve their intended goals of protecting life—instead, they often increase maternal mortality and morbidity while limiting women’s access to preventive care. Countries like Rwanda, which has invested heavily in comprehensive reproductive health services, demonstrate how evidence-based policies can dramatically improve health outcomes.

The path forward requires courage from policymakers to implement evidence-based reforms, even when they challenge traditional viewpoints. The alternative—maintaining policies that demonstrably harm women’s health and limit economic development—becomes increasingly difficult to justify as the evidence mounts.

The Bottom Line

The research delivers a clear message: restrictive abortion laws create a harmful double burden for women, limiting both their ability to prevent unwanted pregnancies and their options if pregnancy occurs. Countries serious about improving maternal health, reducing poverty, and promoting development have a roadmap in this research—comprehensive reproductive health policies that trust women to make informed decisions about their own bodies and futures.

As Sub-Saharan Africa continues to develop economically and socially, reproductive health policy represents both a moral imperative and an economic opportunity. The question isn’t whether change will come, but how quickly countries will embrace evidence-based policies that unlock the potential of half their population.
